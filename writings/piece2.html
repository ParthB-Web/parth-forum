Pain and Joy: The Dichotomy of Life

The Wounded Beauty Essay

There are wounds that don't close. Ever.
Not the kind that aches for bandages –
but the kind that becomes part of your nervous system.

You don't walk away from them.
You build yourself around them.
They twist your shape, your voice, your instinct.
And every feeling you have after them
has a different weight.
A different kind of reality.
A different cost.

I couldn't play the game the way they wanted.
Not because I didn't try.
But because something in me refused to lie.

So I stepped out.
Not in retreat – but in rebellion.

I didn't want the trophy.
I wanted to see the code.
I thought if I could dissect the thing–
life, love, desire, failure and the list goes on,
I'd find some kind of power.
Maybe even salvation.

But all I found was how rigged it all is.
How absurd.
How impossible.

⋆ ⋅ ☾ ⋅ ⋆

But knowing doesn't save you.
It just strips away illusion.
You see too clearly.
You feel too much.

And that's a strange kind of suffering –
being awake in a world that wants to stay asleep.

And yet, even in that,
I still crave love.

Not the kind they sell in movies.
But the kind that knows what ruin smells like.
The kind that doesn't flinch when it sees the blood.
The kind that says,
"I see the wreckage. I'll still sit with you."

And sometimes I feel like –
even that – love…
is nothing but morphine.

For the pain of the deepest wound I carry –
the meaningless void – that I am –
and my awareness of it.
And my heart's yearning for meaning,
to go through the hell that life is.
And for identity –
because without it,
the suffering of this hell is all in vain.
Isn't it?

⋆ ⋅ ☾ ⋅ ⋆

Maybe it's selfish.
But it's not fake.
It doesn't erase the wound –
but when someone sees it,
really sees it –
it doesn't ache in the same way.

It becomes witnessed.
It becomes bearable.
Sometimes, that's all you need.

People think I'm idealistic.
They don't understand.
My idealism isn't naive.
It's earned.

It's born from staring into the abyss,
over and over,
and still hoping there's something sacred
hiding in the pitch-black mess.

Although when I point my flashlight towards it,
it's all empty.
Nothing.
Always.

But this stubbornness doesn't go away.
I need to find something.
I do not want to turn back empty handed,
and live – empty.

⋆ ⋅ ☾ ⋅ ⋆

To me, love is the purest thing there is –
and the second most precious thing after truth.

And by "purity," I don't mean perfection.
I mean the kind of clarity that comes
when two fucked-up, raw, half-healed people
see each other without performance.

When two souls, warped by experience,
still try to collide gently.
That's sacred.
That's real purity.

I test people.
Not to hurt them.
But because I've been hurt.
So I show them my softest parts with quiet dread,
expecting them to leave.

And if they stay, I show more.
Worse.
Deeper.
Until either they break,
or I finally believe.

And if they walk away,
I comfort myself with what I already knew –
that no one stays through all of it.

And when I fail – because I do – I spiral.
I investigate.
I overanalyze.
Not to feel bad,
but to never let it happen again.

I take the hit now
so I won't bleed later.
That's how I survive.
That's how I protect what matters.

⋆ ⋅ ☾ ⋅ ⋆

People don't get it when I say
my greatest joy comes from my deepest pain.

But it's simple.

If you know how truly fucked everything is –
if you carry that knowledge not as an idea
but as a body state –
then any moment of light,
any touch,
any laugh,
feels like a miracle.

It feels unjustified.
Holy.
Real.

And yes, I crave pain sometimes.
Not because I enjoy it –
but because it feels true.
It doesn't lie when it says I matter.
It doesn't try to flatter.

Pain clarifies everything that is me.
The depth of my misery,
and my awareness of it,
shows me my depth –
then I no longer feel dead.

Like how I find everything else – to be dead –
including me, usually.

But then there's joy –
it shows me that I'm not stuck there.
In my mind.
It shows me the fleeting nature of life and time.
Still I cannot help but smile.

—Pain and Joy: The dichotomy of life.

⋆ ⋅ ☾ ⋅ ⋆

I've always believed that my brokenness
had to be justified.
That I needed to be wise,
or beautiful,
or strong because I was hurt.

But maybe the real strength is this:
to not defend the wound.
To just bleed.
And to let someone hold your hand while you do.

If that ever happens –
really happens –
then maybe the wound wasn't a curse.
Maybe it was a doorway.
